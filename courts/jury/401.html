  1. How delay/sluggishness of courts ruin economy

    When a process involves a “spot transaction” between two individual A1 and A2, whose efficiencies are e1 and e2 , the net efficiency will be

    e = e1 e2

    However, if the process is not a “spot transaction” but a step involving delay where one of the two parties makes an investment and then the other party can give the return ONLY after a few days or few weeks, in such a case the efficiency will be

    e = e1 e2 c where c is the efficiency of the court.

    How does “court” come into picture? Because if one party violates the commitment, the other party has no option but to go to the court. Now better the efficiency of the court, harder the party will try to see that contract is met.

    Now consider a process where A1 and A2 deal with each other, but A2 depends on A3 for some of the work. Then net efficiency of such a process, if ALL the transactions are “spot-transactions”

    e = (efficiency of process involving A1, A2) * (efficiency of process involving A2, A3)

    = e1 e2 * e 2 e3

    = e1 e22 e3

    Please note that since A2 deals with two parties, his efficiency gets squared.

    But if the process involves 3 persons (a1, a2, a3) and there is investment and delays in each case, then e = (efficiency of process involving A1, A2) * (efficiency of process involving A2, A3)

    = e1 e2 c * e2e3 c

    = e1 e22 e3 c2 where c is the efficiency of courtroom.

    Likewise, when a process involves 4 persons, and 3 processes (A1, A2), (A2, A3), (A3, A4),

    e = (efficiency of process involving A1, A2) * (efficiency of process involving A2, A3) * (efficiency of process involving A3, A4)

    = (e1 e2 c) * (e2 e3 c) * (e3 e4 c)

    = e1 e22 e32 e4 c3


    In general, when a process involves n persons (A1, A2), (A2, A3), (A3, A4), …. (AN-1 ,, AN),

    e = (efficiency of process involving A1, A2) * (efficiency of process involving A2, A3) * (efficiency of process involving A3, A4) * . . .

    = (e1 e2 c) * (e2 e3 c) * (e3 e4 c) * … * (eN-1 eN c)

    = e1 e22 e32 … eN-1 2 eN cN-1

    In short, of all the efficiencies in the economy, the MOST critical is efficiency of the courts. In many cases, a process will involve 10-20 persons. So N = 20. Thus 1% reduction in efficiency in the courts can cause about 18% reduction in the efficiency in the overall process.

    Now consider countries such as US, UK, New Zealand, China etc with India. By all means, their courts are 2-times more efficient than India’s courts. Due to this difference in the courts’ efficiencies, their economies are 10-50 times more efficient then India’s economy. In fact, in India, if the chain in the production process is longer is than 5-10, that process will never happen and India has to import that product. Best example is semiconductor chips, where production chain is one of the longest. No one in India has yet been able to manufacture semi-conductor chips.

    A fundamental difference between economics and street business is that the subject of economics tend to assume that the parties would keep their promises even in absence of courts. A street businessmen knows that commitment/quality comes by fear of court (or gangsters), demand and supply are not enough.

    2. Power corrupts, and persistent power corrupts with higher probability

      Many lawyers, officers, businessmen, license-seekers etc. would approach a powerful person and offer him favors/bribe, while few would approach a powerless person. Now lets say p is the probability that a person, would come under corrupters’ influences when he is non-corrupt.       So lets say a person is asked to decide upon one case. Then lawyers, criminals etc. involved in the case would try to corrupt him and say chances (probability) he will accept bribe is equal to p. Now after the case is over, chances that person is still non-corrupt is (1 - p). Now lets say same person is asked to resolve one more case. Once again the lawyers, criminals etc. involved in the second case would try to corrupt him. So the chances that the person is non corrupt after second case is (1 - p)(1- p) = (1 - p)2 and chances that he is non-corrupt are 2 cases is [1 - (1 - p)2]. Likewise, probability that a person is non corrupt after M cases is (1 - p)M and that he is corrupt after M cases is [ 1 - (1 - p) M ]. As M increases, [1- (1 - p)M ] increases and reaches close to 100%. Even of p is as small as 0.1% (i.e. 1 in 1000), for M = 2000, i.e. probability that a person would have become corrupt after 2000 instances where he has resolved disputes will be [1 - (1 - p)2000 ] = [1 - (1 - 0.001)2000

      ] = [1 - 0.9992000 ] = [1 - 0.14] = 86%.

    4. Explaining the “unintuitive”

      If probability of a citizen being corrupt is 0.1, then probability of 6 citizens being corrupt in a group of 11 too should be 0.1, how can it be less than 0.1?

      Here is a simple example to explain this fact. Consider a group of 100 citizens where 10 are corrupt. Lets say A1, A2, …. , A10 are corrupt and B1, B2, …. , B90 are honest. The system is to choose one person, and asking him to resolve the dispute, in 1 out 10 cases, there will be corruption. But if the system is to choose 21 individuals and asking them resolve the dispute, there won’t be corruption in even 1 case. Thus if 1 person is chosen, chances of corruption are 0.1, but if 21 persons are chosen, the chances of corruption (for a group of 100) is 0. Likewise, any group of size N, where N/10 are corrupt, if (N/5 + 1) individuals are chosen for decision-making, the chances of corruption will be zero. For group size between 1 and (N/5/ + 1) than chances will be non-zero but lesser than 0.1

    6. Nexus reduces “broadness”, and thus fairness, of opinion

      Lets say there are 7 individuals (a1, a2, a3, … , a7) who are asked to decide and probability of a person being corrupt is 10%. In that case, possibility that 4 or over 4 will be corrupt is 0.27%. But lets say that 3 of

      those 7, say (a1, a2, a3) have nexus i.e. they agree to help each other’s friends/relatives/etc each time a case comes up. In that, the person needs to bribe only one of the three and the other two would comply. In that case, effectively, the group of 7 reduces to a group of 7 - 3 + 1 = 5 and thus probability of corruption increases to 0.86%. And if there are more nexuses, like say (a1, a2, a3,) have nexus and (a4, a5, a6) have nexus, then group size effectively goes down to 7- 3 + 1 - 3 + 1 = 7 - 4 = 3. And so chances of corruption increases to 2.8%. And if the nexus is still stronger, the chances of corruption are much higher.

      Now continuous proximity may breed nexus. Prolonged (several years) of proximity will also increase the strength of the nexus if it already exists. Thus in judge system, where judges are permanent, work together, and so they know and meet each other quite often, the possibility of nexus amongst judges increases. Thus a bench of 11 judges will act like 11 independent members on first day. But after 1 year, continuos proximity may result into formation of nexuses and thus lowering the effective number of independent opinions. In contrast, when 11 citizens are chosen at random from a population of 10,00,000, chances that they know each other are negligible and chances they have nexus are even lower. To be exact, lets say each citizen has 100 close friends/relative. Then chances that at least 2 or more persons know each other in a group chosen from 10,00,000 is 0.001 = 0.1% i.e. 1 in 1000 approximately. Chances that at least 3 or more persons know each other are 0.00001 = 0.001% i.e. 1 in 100,000. Thus nexus amongst Jurors is much much less likely than nexus amongst permanent judges.

    9. Mathematically comparing judge system and Jury System

      Now I will mathematically explain why even if the best humans (that God can make) are made permanent judges, the judge system will have corruption in larger number of cases than in Jury System consisting of mere mortals. Now lets say there are 100,000 disputes every year. Lets say common men are corrupt with 20% probability and society chooses a Jury System of 11 Jurors with majority decision. In such cases, probability that majority will i.e. 6 or more out 11 will be corrupt is 0.017 i.e. 1.7% disputes will have corrupt judgements.

      Now lets give all the benefit of doubt to the judges. Lets say that 100,000 disputes a month are given to 10,000 judges and each resolves 10 cases a month. Lets say that ALL of the 10,000 judges are honest in the beginning, but each time a corrupter (like lawyer, criminal) approaches a judge, the judge may become corrupt with 0.0001 (= 1 in 10000 = 0.01%) probability. And once he becomes corrupt, he will exhibit corruption in over 75% cases he presides. So probability that a judge would corrupt after 1 year, i.e. after presiding 120 cases, is [1 - (1 - 0.0001)120] = 0.01094 = 1.09%. So corruption in judge system will be a less than Jury System in the first year. But at end of 2nd year, [1 - (1 - 0.0001)240] = 2.27% judges would have become corrupt. At the end of 10th year, i.e. 1200 cases each, [1 - (1 - 0.0001)1200] = 11.3% judges would have become corrupt. So after a few years, corruption in judge system is significantly higher than in Jury System.

      That was Maths.

      A simple non-mathematical explanation is : no man in this Universe has infinitely strong integrity ; so even if judges have 1000 times stronger integrity than common men, constant exposure to corrupters will eventually make majority of them corrupt ; and once some of them become corrupt, almost all cases they will preside will involve corruption.

      The common experience relates with above maths. Once upon a time in India, some 50 years ago, very few judges were corrupt. But each year, some 0.5% - 1% of the thousands of non-corrupt judges in lower courts as wells as higher courts became corrupt.   3. Errors vs. Corruption

    An error is when a dispute-resolver (be judge or Juror) declares an innocent person guilty (Type-I error) or declares a guilty person innocent (Type-II errors). A trained and experienced judge will make both errors less compared to a single Juror, but it is turns out that a system with 10 or 11 Jurors where majority decides will have less errors than a judge. For example lets say that a judge will err with probability 0.001 and a Juror will err with probability of 0.10. That is, probability that a common man will err is 100 times more than a judge. I will, later show that chances that 6 or over 6 out of 11 Jurors will err will be 0.0003 which is less than probability that a judge would err. Also, in judge system, a judge has to handle several cases that to concurrently and so chances of errors are higher.

    Even though Jury System reduces error, this plus point is insignificant. The errors are less damaging than bribes. An error would not repeat again and again and could benefit anyone. But corruption/favoritism will repeat with over 95% probability, will be often one-sided and may benefit the same persons again and again. Errors by officers/judges/Ministers/citizens will NOT encourage career criminals, but corruption will encourage career criminals. Thus corruption causes more damage than errors. So the bigger problem in dispute-resolution (i.e. jurisprudence) is NOT error but cash/promotion/fame related corruption.

    In short, even if chances of errors are higher in Jury System due to lack of training, the fact that Jury System is less nexus prone will it make far superior than judge system in a long as well as short run.

  5. More about corruption and nexuses wrt to judge system and Jury System

    1. In the world of corruption, bribes paid in past are useless

      Do people ever respect bribes paid in past? No. Never. Never, never, never.

      In the world of bribes, past is forgotten as if it never happened. A mere glance at policemen’s fake-encounters proves this. The policemen often kill several top-ranking criminals in fake-encounters. At one time, these criminals would have paid handsome cash to all policemen from constables to IGP, secretaries in Home Ministry and Home Minister. But when the policemen decide to kill that criminal, they simply forget all the bribes that the criminal had paid in past and put a bullet in the criminal’s head.

      Likewise, many officers do several favors to businessmen etc. But upon retirement, the businessmen etc. would not even throw a dime at them. As Gujaraties say - “utarto amaldaar kodi no” i.e. a retiring (powerless) officer is worthless. The saying is true not just for retired officer, but for all retired Ministers, retired MLAs, retired MPs, retired judges and retired criminals. The favors done in past never buy any favor in the future. None, except historians, remember history.

    2. What motivates is bribes that will arrive in future, not bribes paid in past

      So payments given in past do not matter. But then, what does matter?

      In hundreds of cases, a party gives cash to second party for goods, service etc. In some cases the cash receiver delivers the goods and in some cases he does not. In general, there are 3 reasons why individuals keep promises - a)future prospects of getting cash/goods from same or other party b)fear of punishment by court c)fear of revenge by private goondas.

      If one dissects corruption and gives a closer look, it would reveal a lesser known fact - a person who takes a bribe, does not do wrong things for the bribe he just took but to ensure that a flow of bribes keep coming in future. Suppose an Income-Tax officer takes a bribe from a tax-evading businessman. Now after taking the bribe, the officer can still arrest the businessman. After all, he does not have to give the bribe back. But if the officer breaks his word and arrests him, he will never get the bribes from same or other businessmen in the future.

      This phenomenon (that “the past is useless and only the future prospects matter”) creates an important difference between net output of Jury System and judge system.

    3. Nexus needs several transactions

      Nexus is an agreement between 2 individuals, which does not have approval of the law and in most cases, is against the law. Essentially, a nexus is an outright illegal understanding.

      Now I will show the following : for two individuals to have a nexus, it is necessary that they have 2 or more transactions. If the situation allows only 1 transaction, nexus is difficult to establish.

      Say the “nexus” is that A will give money to B after B does an action. The question A will face is “if I first give money, then what if B does not do the action”. And the question B will face is “if I first do the action, then what if A does not pay”. Any one who steps forward is investing to create a nexus without any guarantee.

      In case A and B are going to do several transactions, each will be willing to step forward and keep the word. If B executed the action in advance, A will pay the money, otherwise in future B will not do the work. And if A pays in advance, then B will do the work or else A will not pay the money in future.

      This fact is fundamental to Jury System vs judge system debate.

      In Jury, it is known that a Juror will never come in Jury again. So neither the lawyer nor the Juror steps forward to invest into a nexus. The lawyer is worried that if he pays the bribe to the Juror, the Juror still may not keep the word. While the Juror will think that if he delivers the verdict as requested by the lawyer before the lawyer pays the bribe, there is no guarantee that lawyer would pay the bribe later.

      While in the judge system, the lawyer knows that same person will dispense the cases again and again, and so he steps forward to make a nexus. He pays the bribe before the judgement is delivered. Now the judge fears that if he does not deliver the judgement as promised, fewer lawyers would give bribes in future. And so the judge keeps the promise. Gradually the nexuses strengthen.

    4. Corruption in a serious case, without nexus, needs a middleman

      Consider a case where an Income-Tax officer wants to take a bribe from a tax-evading businessman. And the businessman is also willing to pay him a bribe. Even though both parties are ready, there is still a tricky problem - should the beneficiary pay the bribe before the officer does the illegal favor or pay the bribe after the officer does the illegal favor?

      1. if the businessman pays the bribe first, it is possible that the officer would still not close the case, and ask for more bribe later on.

      2. if the officer lets the businessman go first, before the businessman pays the bribe, the businessman may not pay him a penny later on.

      To solve the problem, the businessman and the officer go to a middleman who deals with both the parties on a regular basis. Typically, accountants, lawyers etc work as middlemen. The businessman pays the cash to the accountant/lawyer and only after the officer clears the case, the middleman will pay the cash to the officer. But here it is important to note - the middleman is someone who deals with both the parties on the regular basis, and so there is a future prospect. In short, if two parties are coming in contact for the first time and they don’t have a middleman with whom BOTH interact regularly, none will be willing to do an illegal favor first.

    5. When the bribe-receiver Hates the bribe-giver, the bribe will fail

      A more interesting case is when the person in power intensely Hates the bribe-giver. In such cases, the bribe will fail.

      In academics, the arithmetic of greed is well documented. But the calculus of Hatred, a 100 times more powerful and Godly force, is very poorly documented and under-appreciated. An economist will jump to declare that the person who has power to punish will take the bribe and let the criminal walk away. This only shows that economists don’t take The Hatred into account. The economists’ conclusions often go wrong, and then they blame the common men, saying that they are not “rational”. I disagree. The Hatred in most cases is rational and it is the economist’s folly that he does not take Hatred into account.

      A person who has Hatred, may take the cash but will still act to fulfil his Hatred. Consider Bhim, for example. Bhim had Hatred against Duryodhan and Dushashan. Had Duryodhan offered cash to Bhim, Bhim would have taken it, but even after taking the bribe Bhim would have still killed Duryodhan and Dushashan. Likewise, if Arjun had offered bribes to Karna, Karna would may or may not have taken, but he would have still tried to kill Arjun. In there are only 3 things I noted in the Mahabharat - 1)Arjun could ONLY see the eye of the bird and nothing else 2)Nothing, even money, can overcome The Hatred 3)there is nothing else in The Mahabharat worth noting.

      Even a mountain of gold cannot purchase a man with Hatred.

      If the person does not have Hatred, he would take the bribe and keep the commitment. For example, say a professor takes cash from a student to raise his marks. Now the professor knows that money is not going back, even if he does not raise the marks. But the professor does not have any Hatred against the student anyway. So most likely, he will raise the marks.

      In short, if a person-in-charge has personal Hatred against the bribe-giver, person-in-charge may take the bribe but yet he would act against the bribe-giver. In over 90 out of 100 cases, bribe given to someone who Hates fails.

    6. Why bribe may succeed in judge system but fails in case of Jury System

Now I will come to point - why bribes given to judges succeed in over 90% - 95% cases while bribes given to Jurors fails in the west (and would also fail in India) in over 90% - 95% cases. The reason is -Hatred. The Jurors are common citizens who are hurt by corruption and crimes. So citizens have intense Hatred against criminals and corrupt, but a judge does not. So when the criminal/corrupt pays bribe to a citizen, the citizen will not forget his Hatred. So the Juror may take the bribe, but will still declare him guilty. So statistically, and say if 100 judges were to take bribe over 90 would keep their word to keep flow of future bribes. And so over 90 out of 100 judges who have taken bribe would let the criminal/corrupt walk away. While if 1000 Jurors were to take bribe, over 900 would still vote according to their Hatreds, and so they would not let the criminal or the corrupt walk away.

In short, the judge system has 2 evils : the judge may take a bribe and let a corrupt-officer or a criminal walk away. While the Jury System has only 1 evil - the Jurors may take a bribe but will not let a corrupt officer or criminal walk away.

  3. No career related favors - Many times a judge cannot be bought by cash, but he can be bought by career related favors. The Jurors have no career to be made.
