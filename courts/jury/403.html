  1. Dispute-resolvers and two systems for selecting them : judge system and Jury System

    1. Disputes and Dispute Resolvers

      The societies created courts and “Judicial Procedures” to resolve disputes amongst the citizens. The “Judicial Procedures” are steps a “court” would take to resolve the disputes.

      The first explicit or implicit step that a society takes to resolve a dispute is to appoint dispute-resolvers. The dispute resolvers, are not merely someone who suggest a solution, but someone who can force both parties to obey the solution. Other Judicial steps are - summoning both the parties and witnesses, setting the

      hearing dates, going through the evidences and arguments, declaring the judgement and finally forcing both parties to obey judgement.

      Now lets consider the first Judicial Procedure - choosing the dispute resolvers.

      Two systems have been used in all the countries in past 5000 years to select/appoint dispute-resolvers: system of fixed judges (or judge system or judocracy) and Jury System. I will briefly describe these two systems in this booklet. Other systems to choose dispute-resolvers are combinations of these two basic systems.

      Common wisdom says - judges and Jurors, both may take bribes. So what is better in Jury System? A short answer is -

      1. Jurors are not known in advance and so it is lawyers etc. cannot have nexuses with them.

      2. Jurors (common citizens) have intense Hatred against corrupt officers and criminals; while the judges do not have much Hatred against criminal and corrupt.

      3. Jurors do not face the possibility of loosing bribes in future and have no ambitions

      So it is difficult to bribe majority of Jurors and even in case a Juror takes bribe, he is still very likely to declare a corrupt officer or a guilty person guilty. Of course, this is a short answer and raises several sub-questions. This book answers some of the sub-questions.

    2. The “system of fixed judges” to appoint dispute-resolvers

      What is the “system of fixed judges” or “judge system” or “judocracy” for selecting dispute-resolvers? A system where a small number of individuals are appointed to resolve almost all the disputes in the society. These individuals often have a permanent tenure. And amongst judges, their is a systematic command hierarchy which makes it an organized and established bureaucracy, and hence the term judocracy.

    3. The Jury System for selecting dispute-resolvers

      In the Jury System, when there is a criminal/civil dispute or a complaint against an officer - about 10-15 citizens are chosen at random from the voter-list ; both parties present their case before these citizens ; and finally these citizens give the judgement. So structurally, the difference between two systems is quite minor. Both judge system and Jury System use courts and allow parties to cross question each other.

      The pro-Jury anti-judge folks (like myself) believe - if it is well known in advance that a small group of same persons, i.e. appointed judges, will rule over and over, the lawyers/officers/rich-people will immediately start establishing relations (read nexuses) with the appointed judges. And later on, not everyone, but over 50% to 70% of these appointed persons will become corrupt and nexus-prone. So pro-Jury anti-judge folks (like me) believe that it is best to keep the dispute-resolvers should not be well known and permanent. Analytically, one can show that choosing citizens randomly from the adult population is the best way to avoid nexuses.

      In contrast, the pro-judge anti-Jury folks believe that common men are stupid and more corrupt than elite-members. And so only the chosen-ones, i.e. judges, should resolve the disputes.

      To show that citizens do know the law, I will consider following types of cases
