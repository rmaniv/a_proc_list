There is some 1 judge per population of 75,000 in India. This is low compared to US/UK etc where it is almost 1 judge per less than 10,000 citizens.

This raises two questions for concerned citizens like us, BRites.

1. Why are there so few judges in India?

2. And equally important question is : Whom shall we bash for this short-coming - Neta or judges?

100% blame of this shortage falls on the heads and the shoulders of the high judges (high judges = judges of high and supreme courts). The neta are blameless on THIS SPECIFIC issue.

The high judges have been saying that "we need 60,000 judges in India while we have only 13,000 and that executive (i.e. neta) are not creating 60,000 positions. Well, consider Gujarat for example. We need 6000 judges by western standard. There some 650 judges. But some 150+ positions are lying vacant.

What executive (i.e. neta/babu) say is that once say 125 out of these 150+ vacancies are filled up, we will sanction some 100 more positions. And once high judges have filled those 100 new positions up, we will create 100 more vacancies. Like this, the strength can be increased to a level satisfactory to high judges.

Consider following analogy. Say 1000 people want to travel from X to Y, and that would need say a train with 10 compartments. Say there are only 6 compartments. So do we need to add 4 more? Yes but what if everyday 2 out of these 6 compartments are remaining vacant? That means fault lies elsewhere.

Doesn’t it make sense that high judges should FIRST fill the existing vacant slots and ONLY then ask for slots?

What ails Indian judiciary?

Rahul has raised an interesting topic about Indian judiciary and also suggested a solution. His solution is off course about inducting jury system back. This issue has been discussed in detail in the relevant thread and for the moment I will let it lie.

More important is to understand the importance of judiciary and some fundamental problems with it.

Any modern democratic government is based on rule of law. In fact as the freedom of citizens and the of the enterprise increases, the judicial system (not only judiciary) becomes more important.

Let me give a simplistic example: - if there are no private power companies then there need not be any rule and regulations to control them. On the other hand if there is Private entrepreneurs then the rule of the game has to be made clear.

We normally confuse judicial system with litigation that is incorrect. World wide the litigation has become very small part law and even of legal firms.

Now Indian judicial system is suffering from some severe problems. Like all other institutions it is finding it difficult to address the problem because it is shirking from fundamental issues.

Let us have a bird eye view of the system and the associated points: -

Supreme Court

If any institution suffers from the holier then thou attitude then it is the Supreme Court. There is abject refusal to acknowledge that they are human. In fact most of the problems in Indian system can be laid directly at its door but they refuse to see further than their noses.

The primary duty of the Supreme Court was to deal with Constitutional issues and to co-ordinate the interpretation of law in different High Courts so that there is uniformity of application across India.

It has burdened itself with enormous amount of SLPs running into almost 50-60,000 matters a year most of which are dismissed in limine but drag down more important matters.

So we have constitutional issues settled after decades after the fact.

More importantly it has taken over the administration of justice. So it has almost the final say in the appointment of High Court Judges. As of now there are approx 150 vacancies in the High Court. If we consider the available infrastructure the system can easily absorb around 300 judges. The political system since around 1992 has stopped interfering the appointments.

Now the most important task at hand is to fill these vacancies but instead we have SC almost disinterested. They work around 170 working days in a year and even if they spend one week on the appointments it would do greater good then all the judgments put together.

As an institution it suffers from favoritism, nepotism, indulgence and complete lack of transparency. (Out right corruption -bribes is minimal if not altogether absent)

High Court

HC is the backbone of the judicial system in India. It is supposed to lay down the law that is then to be implemented by Trial (District-Lower) Courts.

The biggest difficulty is the acute shortage of Judges which is self created as discussed above.

The shortage of judges means that the problems snow balls, as the judges do not have time to scrutinize frivolous appeals. Therefore more and more people file appeals.

The problem is compounded even further as no costs are associated with frivolous matters even though the cases are ultimately dismissed. So the appellant takes benefit of the delay and the victim passes on the litigation to the next generation.

If this not enough, the judges are directly flouting the basis of law constituting the appellate courts that they will not interfere with the Judgments of Trial Courts except on major issues. HC interferes on small issues by pettifogging.

The end result is that the HC is snowed under millions of Cases. A matter if admitted takes around 15-25 years to be heard. (After you have been to the trial court that is).

The staff is corrupt, rude & unhelpful and even minor works involves running around or shelling out money.

As an institution it acutely suffers from favoritism, nepotism, indulgence and complete lack of transparency. The litter of Judges practice in the same courts and rest you can imagine.

There are no criteria of appointment of judges and their monitoring. The post of High Court Judge is a constitutional post and a person cannot (practically) be removed from it. But everything is done behind closed doors. So if you want to become a Civil Judge (lowest category) you have a face a written exam and interview. If you want to become a High Court Judge then no such impediment in your way, only thing is that you must know a charmed circle.

(Astoundingly again there is no political interference and everything is in house. Atleast there are something for which we cannot blame the politicians)

District Court & Trial Courts

These courts in which the trial is supposed to take place can be divided into two parts. Criminal Courts (Remember Heller!) and Civil Courts.

Criminal Courts

As usual the first problem is shortage of judges. The next problem is the high degree of corruption in whole criminal judicial system starting from police, prosecutors and judiciary.

A criminal case takes anything between 10-20 years at the trial court level. This is adequate time to break the witnesses, bribe the right people, develop contacts and destroy the evidence.

If you have money you can get away with almost anything. The Nanda case and Jessica lal case are good examples of the way the criminal judicial system is corroding.

Civil Trial Court

The topic starter by Rahul Mehta dealt with basically this branch of law. To be more particular the issues relating to landlord tenants.

I think Mr. Mehta is grumbling needlessly, he is very lucky. If his landlord decides to stop paying rent, claim his house, kill his dog, steal his music system etc even then there is not much he would be able to do is short term cost effective manner. He will have to wait for something like a few decades and let his grandchildren enjoy the fruits of his hard won litigation.

One of the great fallacies propounded are that Indians are compulsive litigators and this burdens the system. This is incorrect. A normal Indian would rarely go for litigation, as no result is ever expected in short or medium term. But the system is such that it rewards and encourages defendants and punishes the person who is trying to claim his right.

Though I donot have personal knowledge, but I was talking to an American attorney who told me that in USA only 1% of the matters go to trial and rest are settled at some or other stage. If all the matters start going to trial then the system will collapse even there. (I am given to understand the system is similar in UK, Singapore, Germany etc also)

There are 3 primary reasons of failure of civil or commercial legal system in India that are as follows: _

1. Costs- the law provides that costs should be provided to the winner of the case for: -

Legal costs incurred
Frivolous defence

But in complete defiance of Indian law and international practices real costs are never granted. So a dishonest person drags you through litigation for 20 years and does not have to pay for it. Hence dishonesty is rewarded and litigation is encouraged.

2. Damages –

Indian courts are some how reluctant to grant real and actual damages. Frivolous reasons and contrite excuses are used to reduce the damages and benefit the wrong doer. I am not talking of exemplary damages as per US law but only real/actual damage.

Let me give an example- Why was the US company so desperate to bring the case to India in regards to Bhopal, the reason that damages are miniscule.

More run of the example is a recent case in the Supreme Court where an X corporate giant was involved in case in which they organized a function in defiance of Government directions. Even the pandal did not fulfill the safety regulations. Due to fire numerous members of the audience were burned alive.

The Supreme Court reached a conclusion that for roasting alive a young child the company is not liable to pay damages. But in all benevolence it awarded around (a high) US$ 1500 per child (and mind you these were well off middle class people). This is amazingly low by Indian standards also.

These damages were awarded as the Company X conceded its liability and decided to co-operate. Therefore it only took 6 years for the huge amount of damages.

In contested personal injury damage cases, only thing one should expect in couple of decades is “nothing or next to nothing”.

This type of reluctance to give real damages is prevalent in all branches of law (for instance land lord tenant matters) giving benefit to wrongdoer. Additionally this increases litigation, as the wrong doer never compromises, as he knows that litigation is beneficial. And if some Judge does award damages then the appellate court needlessly intervenes setting off another round of litigation.

3. Interest

Due to quirks of Indian system the courts only award “simple” rate of interest. Let me give you an example. Mr. D owes Rs. 100,000/- to Mr. P. he does not pay. P sues D. D does not raise any tangible defence except blatant denial. It takes 2 decades to win the matter and recover the money.

D will have to pay say 100,000/- plus simple rate of interest of ~12% for 20 years = 240,000/- plus costs say 25,000/- plus his own costs say 20,000/- totaling Rs. 385,000/-

Now we are assuming P gets ~385,000/- less his real costs of litigation and running around and mental agony. The costs of genuine Plaintiff are always more than defendant. So we say his costs are around 50,000/-. Ultimately he gets 335,000/- after 20 years. Without compensation for running around or for mental agony of fighting a frivolous matter.

If the same money was borrowed from bank the interest is around ~15% compounded quarterly which means the total sum should be around ~20 lakhs. So D saves ~16 lakhs after loosing the case.

If P had invested even in FDR with annual compounding at ~12% I think (calculation?) he would have made ~10 lakhs. So after winning the case he lost ~7 lakhs or thereabouts

So the system encourages litigation by amply rewarding the wrong doer. And this is an example where he (D) actually looses. Chances are he may win.

The CPC (Amendment) Act 2002 fixes a time limit for every stage of litigation, starting with the filing of a suit to the delivery of judgement.....

The changes in the CPC will also make it extremely difficult to drag civil cases either through convoluted arguments or by quoting procedure.

According to Vikas Pahwa, lawyer, "Now the delaying tactics used by the lawyers and the litigants in various cases can be avoided. And the time spent on unnecessary arguments will be cut down."

With the amended Civil Procedure Code, people involved in property disputes, landlord-tenant cases and money disputes can look forward to a faster trial. And the lawyer fraternity is hoping for a similar amendment in the Criminal Procedure Code also to ensure justice for all.

The Chief Justice of the Punjab and Haryana High Court strips three Judges of the court of work for their alleged role in the scandal involving former Punjab Public Service Commission chief Ravi Sidhu.

PRAVEEN SWAMI

WITH its massive concrete walls, the Punjab and Haryana High Court resembles nothing so much as a fortress, built to secure justice from scrutiny by the world outside. Sidhu had organised the manipulation of mark-sheets and the pre-delivery of examination papers to help Justice Amarbir Gill's daughter, Amol Gill, and Justice Singhal's daughter, Sapna Singhal, gain government jobs.

Justice Mehtab Gill's role was even more serious. A childhood friend of Sidhu, the Judge funnelled several candidates through Sidhu's enterprise of fraud.
