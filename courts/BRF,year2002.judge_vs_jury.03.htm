It is highly likely that a jury system would add to the judicial problems in India.

From a system perspective it adds complexity. The current system has several actors in it already such as the police, prosecutor, defendant, witnesses and judge. This would add yet another set of actors - a jury of say 12 people. In the USA all tweleve have to vote unanimously for a verdict.

Even in westerm countries jury system has created numerous problems, all of which would be compounded in India:

1. Jury tampering in the USA used to occur (remember how the judge changed Al Capone's jury at the last minute to prevent a tampered jury from being empanelled). Twelve people in India would be 12 additional sources of tampering a trial. Under US rules all you need to do is to tamper with just one juror.

2. A very large number of appeals in the USA are related to the jury. For example appeals are filed on the basis of the nature of instructions to jury, whether jurors filled out forms correctly, whether jurors were improperly included or excluded, whether what happened inside jury rooms constituted coercion etc. These kinds of problems would be compounded in India - Did the jurors understand the issues? Did the Did the jurors indicate the correct occupation? Did the jurors fill out the forms correctly? These would all be basis for needless appeals.

3. A common source of manipulation of justice in Indian courts is in the scheduling of cases. People use a variety of mechanisms such as bribery or influence to get a case from being assigned a date or vice versa. Now consider a case that lasts for more than one day and now additional opportunity is created to obstruct justice by manipulating jurors who declare themselves sick or do not show up.

4. Jury adds greater considerable complexity to adjudication of cases:

- Systems have to be created when a jury needs to be sequestered. This means decent hotel rooms, transportation, arrangement of meals and controling the access to information such as newspapers and tv. In many small towns even these basic arrangements are not easy.

- In sensitive cases, jury needs security. Currently, judges who deal with mobsters and terrorists are provided with security. Now this additional security has to be provided to the jury, many of whom are likely to live in areas where it is really quite hard to do.

- System have to exist so that simple things such as the citizenship of a juror can be verified. How many in India have birth certificates? Systems also need to be in place so that the same person is not called for jury duty more than a certain number of times. Jurors are paid. This means that a hassle free system of payments exists. Imagine being a juror in India and being handed a voucher and now you are running around clerks seeking payment.

- Jury system itself needs to policed. Swift justice is meted out when a juror is found to be corrupt. Failing this the jury system would itself disintegrate into judicial chaos. In India, where justice moves slowly and numerous means exist to delay the dispensation of justice, it is highly likely that the jury system would itself be quickly mired in corruption.

In Western countries the jury system works because there is an educated populace and there is an organized society in place to handle the additional complexity of having jurors sit in on trials.

In India, our rules, regulations and procedures need to be simplified because we don't have systems to handle greater complexity. We need more simplicity not greater complexity. A jury would add yet another layer of complexity to a system of justice thereby making it even more cumbersome and susceptible to manipulation.
